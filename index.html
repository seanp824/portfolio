01

Algorithm Visualizer

Making invisible computer science visible, in the browser.

Try it live

The problem. Algorithms are taught as pseudocode and big-O tables, so students memorize behavior they've never actually seen.

The calls I made. Instead of one polished demo, I built a single consistent surface for the whole first-year CS curriculum: pathfinding, sorting, and eight data structures from stacks to AVL and splay trees, all sharing the same visualize/reset/speed controls. Built with Next.js, React, and TypeScript, with direct manipulation: you place the start, the end, and the barriers yourself.

What shipped. A live, public visualizer anyone can use without an account or a setup step.

02

PlayForge

A playbook designer for coaches: draw it, animate it, share it.

Try it live

The problem. Plays get drawn on whiteboards and die in the locker room. A diagram on paper can't show timing, and timing is the whole play. Coaches needed something players could replay on the phone in their pocket.

The calls I made. Animation is the product, not a feature: a play is motion, so routes and formations animate with controllable speed instead of sitting as static arrows. I built one canvas model that serves football, basketball, and soccer rather than three separate tools, and kept it in the browser so it works on whatever device a coach or player already has.

What shipped. A live designer with sport palettes, play animation, playbooks, and team sharing.

03

Rubik's Cube Solver

A solver built from scratch, no solving library imported.

View the code

The problem. Existing solver libraries hand you the answer and teach you nothing. I wanted to own the whole pipeline: from a messy physical cube in your hands to a move-by-move solution you can actually follow.

The calls I made. I implemented the beginner's method myself in Python instead of importing Kociemba. For input I built two paths: type the cube state manually, or scan each face with a webcam through OpenCV. The model of the cube, the move logic, and the solving stages are all hand-rolled.

What shipped. A working solver on GitHub that takes any scramble and produces the steps to solve it. I'm now evolving it into a mobile app with camera scanning and a 3D guided solve, taking the same engine from terminal to pocket.
